Project Overview

MSCHF — the chaos machine.

MSCHF is a Brooklyn-based art collective turned product company — famous for Big Red Boots, Jesus Shoes, and dropping internet-breaking products every few weeks. Their existing site was functional but failed to match the pure unhinged energy of the brand.

This is a concept redesign that amplifies what MSCHF already is — chaotic, bold, culturally aggressive — while improving usability, navigation, and the drop-buying experience for real users.

Problem & goals

01

The Problem

The original MSCHF site felt like a generic Shopify store wearing a costume. The brand's chaos and cultural weight didn't translate — product pages were bland, drops got buried, and the mobile experience was broken.

02

The Goals

Design a platform that FEELS like MSCHF — aggressive, editorial, drop-first. Build urgency into every page. Create a component system that scales with new drops weekly. Make mobile the primary experience.

Design process

A structured approach — but with chaos as a core design principle. Every decision asked: does this feel like MSCHF?

01
Brand Audit
Analyzed MSCHF's visual language, product history, social presence, and competitor drop platforms. Mapped the gap between brand identity and site experience.
02
Information Architecture
Restructured navigation around drops — not categories. Defined user flows for first-time visitors, collectors, and repeat buyers. Drop urgency is the primary driver.
03
Design System
Built a complete component library — cards, drop badges, countdown timers, product carousels, lookbook grid, and form elements. Yellow / black / white with red urgency.
04
Screen Design
Designed 20+ screens across desktop and mobile — homepage, drops listing, individual product pages, lookbook, contact form, and footer system.

Key Learnings

What I learned.

01
Brand chaos is a feature
MSCHF's chaotic energy is the product. The design job is channeling chaos into intentional UX — not taming it. Wild aesthetics can coexist with clear hierarchy.
02
Urgency drives drops
Every element on a drop page must create FOMO. Countdown timers, low-stock badges, and limited-access messaging aren't decorative — they're functional conversion tools.
03
Mobile is the product
Drop culture is mobile-native. Desktop is secondary. Every layout decision starts with how it feels on a phone screen at 2am during a drop.
04
Systems scale drops
A proper component library that can support any product — boots, NFTs, albums, collabs — is the real design challenge. One-off screens don't scale with 52 drops a year.
